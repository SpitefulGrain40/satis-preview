My read: Spring #2fce85 is the most on-brand — it's literally sage's hue with the saturation turned up, so a tick feels like "the brand, energised" rather than a borrowed UI green. Emerald #10b981 is the next best — deeper and jewel-like, sits closest to the forest greens. Pump green #22c55e gives the most continuity with the old app and is the most universally "this means done," but it's a touch cooler/more generic next to the warm forest. All three work; it comes down to whether you value on-brand harmony (Spring), richness (Emerald), or continuity (Pump green). Whichever wins becomes a reserved --success token, used only for completion/positive states — sage stays the brand.

★ Refinements to review

Three open questions rendered for a decision: how a positive result should feel significant, how deep the accent card should be, and a consistent numbers-vs-lettering rule.

1 · Making a "positive" feel significant

You're right — sage text on the dark palette is too quiet to read as a win. On a dark theme, significance comes from fill + brightness, not a text-colour swap. Options A→E, quietest to loudest:

My recommendation — a tiered approach: D (vibrant forest fill) as the standard "positive" treatment — it answers your instinct exactly (bright forest, stays in-family, reads clearly). Reserve E (fill + glow) for rare milestones so it keeps its impact. Use B (moss text) for inline/minor positives where a full card is too much. This keeps plain sage text out of the "win" job entirely.

3 · Numbers vs lettering — a consistent rule

Proposed rule: all numerals → Cormorant Garamond; all letters (labels, units, words, axis text) → system font. This is a deliberate editorial pairing — serif figures + sans labels — and it gives every number a quiet brand signature. Units like "kg" stay system, so "102.4 kg" mixes within a single value.
